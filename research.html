Ongoing Research & Projects

My research integrates microbiology, plant biotechnology, and practical cultivation methods to develop eco-friendly and sustainable crop improvement strategies that benefit both farmers and the environment.

1. Farm Mechanization & Practical Field Solutions

Development of generalized, low-cost mechanization tools and techniques aimed at improving traditional farming systems, increasing operational efficiency, and reducing manual labor across various crops.

2. Disease Management in Rice and Solanaceae Crops

Research on controlling major diseases in rice and Solanaceae crops (such as tomato, brinjal, and chili) using biocontrol agents and novel formulation techniques.

  • Targeted pathogens include sheath blight, blast, bacterial leaf blight, false smut, wilt, and leaf spot diseases.
  • Use of native strains of *Trichoderma*, *Pseudomonas fluorescens*, and *Bacillus* species.
  • Development of biopolymer-based encapsulated bioformulations for increased stability and delivery.
  • Study of protein-pathogen interactions to understand suppression mechanisms. These strategies are a future-ready approach to sustainable disease control in both field and horticultural crops.

3. PGPR-Based Crop Enhancement & Product Development

Exploration of Plant Growth-Promoting Rhizobacteria (PGPR) for improving plant performance.

  • Nutrient solubilization, abiotic stress tolerance, and enhanced plant growth.
  • Integration of secondary metabolites and bioagents in formulation design.
  • Development of next-generation PGPR-based products combining beneficial microbes, bioformulations, and microbial metabolites. These technologies aim to replace or reduce chemical inputs while supporting long-term soil health and crop yield.

4. CRISPR-Cas9 Genome Editing for Crop Improvement

Use of CRISPR-Cas9 gene editing in crops like tomato and other Solanaceae family members to improve yield potential, resistance to fungal and bacterial pathogens, and stress resilience (e.g., drought, heat). This work focuses on developing genetically improved varieties that are farmer-friendly, resource-efficient, and aligned with modern agricultural demands.

5. New Variety Development

Ongoing efforts to develop new crop varieties with improved genetics for better adaptability, resilience, and productivity.

  • Selection of high-performing lines through microbial and genomic support.
  • Combining traditional breeding with modern tools like PGPR and gene editing. The goal is to offer stable, high-quality varieties suited for both open-field and protected cultivation systems.

6. Hydroponic Cultivation for Leafy Vegetables

Design and setup of low-cost hydroponic systems for cultivating leafy greens such as spinach, lettuce, and amaranthus in clean, soil-free environments. Work focuses on nutrient optimization using microbial or organic inputs, promoting sustainable, space-efficient food production for urban and peri-urban areas, and supporting fresh, chemical-free vegetable supply chains.
